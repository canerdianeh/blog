After a brief respite, the Working From Home series is back, as it looks like a lot of people are going to be doing this for a while longer. Some of you, maybe permanently. in this post, I'll talk about sound.

One of the most common thing you will do when working from home is calls and conferences. And from what I've noticed since everyone went to working from home is how utterly bad the default audio devices (input AND output) are on most computers. Cameras aren't much better, but those are really beyond the scope of this post - most seasoned teleworkers rarely use their camera.

As is the case with any kind of signals, whether it's audio or Wi-Fi, it ultimately boils down to maximising signal, and minimising noise. And a big part of reducing the noise in an audio system is the physical space.

Acoustics

Acoustics deals with how sound behaves in the physical space. Sound waves are waves of subtle air pressure changes. We perceive sound when those air pressure changes cause motion of small hairs in our ears and trigger nerves. How those air movements reach our ears changes how we hear them. The vibration frequency of these waves is perceives as pitch, and the intensity of the pressure changes is perceived as volume. Too much volume can cause significant damage.

A basic principle of waves (whether it's a pressure wave or an electromagnetic one) is how they interact with other objects. depending on the angle of arrival, it will do two things: Some of the energy will bounce off (reflection), and some will be absorbed and cause the object to start vibrating (which can be transmitted to other objects attached to it, or back to the air on the other side.

Because sound waves originate from a point source and radiate outward, a wave will behave very differently if the object it encounters is flat, and whether it absorbs or reflects will depend on both the angle of arrival and the density of the material. Dense materials require more energy to cause them to vibrate, and thus will tend to reflect more. The more energy that is reflected away is more sound bouncing around the space. When a reflected wave gets back to our ear at a different time than the original sound, that is known as echo. Larger spaces can cause a series of echos if sound isn't absorbed (and due to air friction and absorption, it does lose some energy along the way, causing it to eventually fade).

On a conference call, echo adds to the noise level that others are hearing, and makes it more difficult to hear. There are two ways to reduce the amount of echo in your workspace: Reducing reflection (and increase absorption), and reducing the intensity (volume) of the sound.

Reducing reflection is tricky in a residential or office space - we tend to work in neat square or rectangular boxes, and echos bouncing back and forth between parallel walls (known as slapback) can make for a lot of interference. The easiest way is to cover the wall (or parts of it) with a softer material, and increase the amount of texture. This is most commonly done with "egg crate" foam. It's relatively cheap, easy to put up, and very effective. Because it's made of spongy foam, it also transmits very little of the absorbed energy to the wall behind it (and vice versa), which is very helpful when the wall goes to space where there are other people and sources of noise.

An often overlooked source of reflection and transmitted noise are the ceiling and the floor... If you go into any commercial office, you'll notice that the floor is often covered in carpet, and there are textured tiles on the ceiling. These are used primarily for acoustic reasons - the carpet to reduce reflections off a concrete floor, and the ceiling tiles to reduce reflections off the ceiling as well as noise from mechanical systems and the floor above.

The nearly ubiquitous "drop ceiling" with square or rectangular tiles is an important part of the acoustic treatment of a space - each tile is made of a soft material and has a textured surface (which increases absorption and scatters reflection, and floats freely within its grid square (which significantly reduces how much absorbed energy it transfers to the support grid, and also makes for easier maintenance access above the finished ceiling . The grid is then itself suspended from structure via thin wires that reduce vibrations even more.
